Around 1550–1525 BC

Ahmose I, an influential Egyptian pharaoh, reigned during the New Kingdom's early 18th Dynasty, around 1550–1525 BCE. He played a pivotal role in the expulsion of the Hyksos, a foreign group that had controlled parts of Egypt for several decades. Ahmose I's military campaigns successfully liberated the Nile Delta and the rest of Egypt, marking the end of the Second Intermediate Period. His reign initiated the New Kingdom's era of prosperity and expansion, characterized by significant cultural and architectural advancements. Ahmose I is also renowned for consolidating power, restoring traditional Egyptian religious practices, and establishing a stable foundation for future pharaohs. His contributions laid the groundwork for the greatness of the New Kingdom, which would include the illustrious reigns of subsequent pharaohs like Thutmose III and Ramesses II.

1541–1520 BC

Amenhotep I, an Egyptian pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, ruled during the early 16th century BCE. Succeeding his father Ahmose I, Amenhotep I continued the work of consolidating power and stability in post-Hyksos Egypt. His reign is characterized by military successes against Nubia, securing the southern borders of the Egyptian kingdom. Amenhotep I also implemented administrative reforms, contributing to the centralization of authority. Despite the brevity of his reign, he is remembered for his focus on cultural and religious endeavors, including the construction of monuments and temples. Amenhotep I laid the groundwork for the subsequent powerful rulers of the New Kingdom, leaving a legacy of political and cultural continuity during a crucial period of Egyptian history.

1520–1492 BC

Thutmose I, a prominent pharaoh of the early 18th Dynasty in ancient Egypt, ruled from approximately 1506 to 1493 BCE. He ascended to the throne after the death of his father, Amenhotep I, and is best known for his military exploits and efforts to expand the Egyptian empire. Thutmose I conducted successful military campaigns in Nubia, consolidating Egyptian control over the region. His reign marked a continuation of the New Kingdom's expansion and prosperity, laying the foundation for the powerful reigns that would follow. Thutmose I also initiated construction projects, including temples and tombs, contributing to the architectural legacy of the New Kingdom. His military achievements and strategic leadership set the stage for the reigns of his successors, including the renowned Thutmose III, who would further solidify Egypt's dominance in the ancient world.

1492–1479 BC

Thutmose II, the fourth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty in ancient Egypt, reigned from around 1493 to 1479 BCE. He was the son of Thutmose I and married his half-sister, Hatshepsut, who later became one of Egypt's most famous pharaohs. Thutmose II's rule was relatively short, and he is often overshadowed by the accomplishments of his wife. Nevertheless, he continued military campaigns in Nubia, maintaining Egyptian control over the southern territories. His premature death led to a succession crisis, eventually resulting in Hatshepsut assuming the role of regent for Thutmose III, the young heir to the throne. Thutmose II's reign is significant for its transitional nature, setting the stage for the remarkable era that followed under the joint rule of Hatshepsut and Thutmose III.

1479–1458 BC

Hatshepsut, one of the most notable pharaohs of ancient Egypt, reigned from around 1479 to 1458 BCE during the 18th Dynasty of the New Kingdom. She is particularly renowned for breaking with tradition and assuming the full powers of a pharaoh, a role typically reserved for men. Initially serving as regent for her stepson, Thutmose III, Hatshepsut gradually took on the responsibilities of kingship and eventually declared herself pharaoh. During her rule, Egypt experienced a period of prosperity marked by extensive trade, artistic and architectural achievements, including the construction of the famous temple at Deir el-Bahari. Hatshepsut's reign was a time of relative peace, and she is remembered as one of the most successful and influential pharaohs in ancient Egyptian history. However, her legacy faced some attempts at erasure after her death, likely due to the unconventional nature of her rule, but modern archaeology and historical research have restored her to a well-deserved place of prominence in Egyptian history.

1458–1425 BC

Thutmose III, often referred to as the "Napoleon of Egypt," was the sixth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty and ruled Egypt from around 1479 to 1425 BCE. Initially overshadowed by his stepmother and co-regent, Hatshepsut, Thutmose III assumed sole rule after her death. He is widely recognized for his military prowess, conducting numerous successful campaigns that expanded the Egyptian empire to its greatest territorial extent. The Battle of Megiddo in 1457 BCE is particularly famous, earning Thutmose III a reputation as a brilliant strategist. Despite his military focus, Thutmose III also contributed to the cultural and architectural development of Egypt, sponsoring construction projects and overseeing the building of temples. His reign is considered a high point in ancient Egyptian history, marking the zenith of the New Kingdom. Thutmose III's efforts in both military and cultural spheres left an enduring legacy, and he is remembered as one of Egypt's most accomplished pharaohs.

1425–1400 BC

Amenhotep II, the seventh pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, reigned over Egypt from around 1427 to 1401 BCE during the New Kingdom period. The son of Thutmose III, Amenhotep II continued his father's military campaigns and sought to maintain the territorial gains achieved during Thutmose's reign. He is best known for his military successes in campaigns against the kingdom of Mitanni and the rebellious city-states in Syria. Amenhotep II also undertook extensive building projects, contributing to the construction and restoration of temples and monuments. His reign is characterized by both military achievements and cultural developments, with a particular emphasis on athletic activities and the promotion of a martial image. Amenhotep II's rule, while not as transformative as some of his predecessors, played a crucial role in consolidating the New Kingdom's power and preserving the empire established by Thutmose III.

1400–1390 BC

Thutmose IV, the eighth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, ruled Egypt from approximately 1401 to 1391 BCE during the New Kingdom period. His reign is notable for a famous dream stela located between the paws of the Sphinx at Giza, in which he claimed that the Sphinx had promised him the throne if he cleared the sand covering its body. Thutmose IV is credited with restoring the Sphinx and, as a result, he is sometimes referred to as the "Sphinx Pharaoh." Despite the focus on the Sphinx, Thutmose IV's reign is generally considered less militarily active compared to his predecessors. He engaged in some campaigns in Nubia and the Levant, but his reign is marked more by diplomatic efforts and internal development. Thutmose IV's son, Amenhotep III, would later ascend to the throne and preside over a period of great prosperity and artistic achievement in ancient Egypt.

1390–1352 BC

Amenhotep III, the ninth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from around 1386 to 1353 BCE during the New Kingdom period. His reign is often regarded as a time of unprecedented prosperity, peace, and cultural flourishing. Amenhotep III's reign saw remarkable artistic and architectural achievements, with a particular emphasis on grand construction projects and the development of a distinct artistic style. The pharaoh commissioned the construction of numerous temples, statues, and monuments, including the Colossi of Memnon and the Luxor Temple. He engaged in extensive diplomatic relations and trade, maintaining stable international relations and accumulating vast wealth. The reign of Amenhotep III is sometimes referred to as the "Golden Age of Egypt," reflecting the wealth and cultural richness of this period. His son, Akhenaten, would later succeed him and bring about significant religious and cultural changes in ancient Egypt.

1352–1336 BC

Akhenaten, originally known as Amenhotep IV, was the tenth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, ruling over ancient Egypt from around 1353 to 1336 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He is perhaps best known for his religious reforms, notably the promotion of Aten, the sun disc, as the sole deity to be worshipped. This marked a departure from the traditional polytheistic religious beliefs of ancient Egypt. Akhenaten moved the capital from Thebes to a new city called Akhetaten (modern-day Amarna), emphasizing the shift in religious focus.
Akhenaten's reign had profound effects on Egyptian art, introducing a more naturalistic and expressive style. The depiction of the pharaoh and the royal family became more intimate and less formal. Nefertiti, Akhenaten's wife, played a prominent role in both the religious and artistic changes.
Despite the initial enthusiasm for the Aten cult, Akhenaten's reforms faced resistance, and after his death, traditional religious practices were reinstated. His successor, Tutankhamun (formerly Tutankhaten), reversed the religious reforms and restored the worship of traditional deities. Akhenaten's legacy is complex, remembered both for his revolutionary religious ideas and the subsequent rejection of his reforms by later pharaohs.

There is some uncertainty and mystery surrounding Smenkhkare's identity and the exact nature of his rule. He is often considered a successor or co-regent of Akhenaten and may have been a brother or close relative.
Smenkhkare's reign is thought to have been brief, possibly lasting for only a few years. He is associated with the religious changes initiated by Akhenaten, continuing the worship of the sun disc Aten. Some theories propose that Smenkhkare and Nefertiti, Akhenaten's queen, may have co-ruled for a time. The historical records are fragmented, and much about Smenkhkare's role, relationships, and the circumstances of his rule remains unclear. His reign is a subject of ongoing scholarly debate and investigation within the field of Egyptology.

1332–1324 BC

Tutankhamun, often colloquially referred to as "King Tut," was an Egyptian pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty who reigned from around 1332 to 1323 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He ascended to the throne at a very young age, possibly around nine or ten years old. Tutankhamun is most famous for the intact burial of his tomb, discovered by Howard Carter in the Valley of the Kings in 1922. The discovery of the tomb was a monumental event in archaeology, as it provided an unprecedented glimpse into the funerary practices, art, and artifacts of ancient Egypt.
Tutankhamun's reign was relatively short, and he is believed to have died around the age of 18. His parentage remains a subject of debate, but he is commonly thought to be the son of Akhenaten and one of his sisters. During his reign, Tutankhamun reversed the religious reforms instituted by his predecessor Akhenaten, restoring the worship of traditional Egyptian gods.
While Tutankhamun's historical significance during his lifetime may have been limited, the discovery of his tomb and its treasures has made him one of the most famous pharaohs in popular culture. The artifacts from his burial, including the iconic golden death mask, have become symbols of ancient Egypt's wealth and artistic achievement.

1324–1320 BC

Ay was an influential figure during the later years of the 18th Dynasty in ancient Egypt. He served as a high-ranking official under Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, and possibly Ay's wife, Queen Nefertiti. After Tutankhamun's death, Ay succeeded him as pharaoh, ruling from around 1323 to 1319 BCE.
Ay's reign was relatively short, and during his time, he worked to undo the religious reforms of Akhenaten and return Egypt to traditional polytheistic beliefs. Ay's relationship with Tutankhamun is a subject of scholarly debate, with some suggesting a familial connection, possibly through marriage ties.

1320–1292 BC

Horemheb, the last pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from around 1319 to 1292 BCE. Before ascending to the throne, Horemheb served in the military under Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, and Ay. His military career played a crucial role in the political landscape of the time, and he rose to the position of commander-in-chief of the army.
Horemheb came to power after Ay, and his reign marked a return to traditional Egyptian religious practices and political stability. He undertook extensive building projects, including the completion of the temple at Karnak, and worked to restore monuments that had been defaced during Akhenaten's religious reforms.
Horemheb's efforts helped stabilize the country and set the stage for the subsequent 19th Dynasty. He had no direct heir, and after his death, the throne passed to Ramesses I, marking the beginning of a new era in ancient Egyptian history. Horemheb's reign is often seen as a period of restoration and recovery after the challenges faced during the Amarna Period.

1320–1292 BC

Ramesses I, also known as Paramessu, was the founder of the 19th Dynasty of ancient Egypt. He ruled for a relatively short period, from around 1292 to 1290 BCE. Ramesses I came to the throne after the death of Horemheb, the last pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty.
Before becoming pharaoh, Ramesses I served as a high-ranking military official under Horemheb. His brief reign was marked by efforts to consolidate power and stabilize the country after the political and religious upheavals of the preceding centuries. Ramesses I initiated building projects, but his short time on the throne limited the scope of his contributions to the long-term development of Egypt.
Ramesses I's most significant legacy is the establishment of a new royal line, as he was the grandfather of the more famous Ramesses II, often referred to as Ramesses the Great. The 19th Dynasty, which began with Ramesses I, played a crucial role in shaping the history and power of ancient Egypt during the New Kingdom period.

1290–1279 BC

Seti I, the second pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1290 to 1279 BCE during the New Kingdom period. Seti I was the son of Ramesses I, the founder of the dynasty, and his reign is notable for its military campaigns, building projects, and efforts to restore Egypt's power and prestige.
Seti I conducted military expeditions in various regions, including campaigns against the Hittites in Anatolia. His military successes contributed to the stability and security of Egypt during his rule. Seti I also undertook extensive construction projects, including the construction of the Great Hypostyle Hall at the Karnak Temple in Thebes, where he adorned the walls with detailed reliefs depicting scenes of his military victories and religious offerings.
One of Seti I's most famous achievements is the construction of his tomb in the Valley of the Kings, known as KV17. The tomb is renowned for its elaborate decorations and well-preserved reliefs. Seti I's rule set the stage for the remarkable reign of his son, Ramesses II, who would become one of Egypt's most celebrated pharaohs.

1279–1213 BC

Ramesses II, commonly known as Ramesses the Great, was the third pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty and one of the most powerful and influential rulers in ancient Egypt. He reigned for an exceptionally long period, from around 1279 to 1213 BCE during the New Kingdom period. Ramesses II was the son of Seti I and Queen Tuya.
His reign is often characterized by military campaigns, monumental building projects, and diplomatic endeavors. Ramesses II engaged in military expeditions against the Hittites, most notably at the Battle of Kadesh, a conflict that ended in a stalemate but was later commemorated through inscriptions and reliefs that exaggerated the pharaoh's achievements. Despite the lack of a clear victory, the peace treaty that followed marked diplomatic success and established a lasting peace between Egypt and the Hittite Empire.
Ramesses II is perhaps best known for his ambitious construction projects, including the construction of the Ramesseum and the temples at Abu Simbel. The latter features colossal statues of Ramesses II carved into the rock facade. His numerous monuments and inscriptions aimed to glorify his reign and emphasize his military prowess.
In addition to his military and architectural achievements, Ramesses II is believed to be the pharaoh mentioned in the biblical story of the Exodus, although historical evidence for this is limited. After his death, Ramesses II was buried in the Valley of the Kings, and his mummy is one of the best-preserved pharaonic mummies ever discovered. Ramesses II's long and prosperous reign left an enduring mark on Egypt's history, earning him the epithet "the Great."

1213–1203 BC

Merneptah, the thirteenth son of Ramesses II, was the fourth pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty, ruling over ancient Egypt from around 1213 to 1203 BCE during the New Kingdom period. His reign followed that of his father, Ramesses II. Merneptah's accession to the throne marked the end of an era of great pharaohs, and he faced challenges in maintaining the empire built by his predecessors.
One of the notable events during Merneptah's reign was a military campaign against the Libyans and the mysterious "Sea Peoples." He left an account of this campaign on the Merneptah Stele, which is famous for containing the earliest known mention of the people identified as Israel. The stele claims a victory over various enemies, including the Israelites.
Merneptah also undertook building projects, including the construction of a palace in Memphis, the restoration of temples, and the completion of monuments initiated by his father. His mortuary temple in Thebes, known as the Merneptah Mortuary Temple, is one of the structures attributed to him.

1203–1200 BC

Amenmesse was a pharaoh who ruled over Egypt during the late 19th Dynasty, approximately around 1203 to 1200 BCE. His reign is considered one of the more enigmatic periods in ancient Egyptian history. Amenmesse's precise relationship to the royal family and the circumstances of his rule remain uncertain.
It is believed that Amenmesse seized the throne after the death of Merneptah, possibly in the midst of a succession dispute. Some scholars propose that Amenmesse may have been a half-brother or a distant relative of Merneptah. The uncertainty surrounding his legitimacy is reflected in the conflicting historical accounts, and the exact nature of his rule remains unclear.
Amenmesse's reign was relatively short, and he is often associated with a period of disorder and political instability. His successor, Seti II, eventually restored stability to the throne. Amenmesse's historical legacy is marked by the challenges and uncertainties surrounding his rule rather than any significant accomplishments or contributions to Egyptian history.

1203–1197 BC

Seti II, the son of Merneptah, was the fifth pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty, ruling over Egypt from around 1200 to 1194 BCE during the New Kingdom period. His reign followed the brief and somewhat uncertain rule of Amenmesse. Seti II worked to restore stability to the throne and the country after the perceived disorder of the previous period.
II faced challenges from the remnants of Amenmesse's faction and had to assert his authority to secure the throne. His rule included military campaigns against Libyan invaders, seeking to maintain control over Egypt's borders. Seti II also conducted building projects, including the construction of a temple at Abydos, where he sought to emphasize his legitimacy by associating himself with the accomplishments of his forebears.
Seti II's reign was relatively short, and he was succeeded by his son, Merneptah Siptah. The political climate during Seti II's rule, marked by succession disputes and regional challenges, reflects the declining power of the New Kingdom and the increasing instability that would characterize the subsequent Third Intermediate Period in ancient Egyptian history.

1197–1191 BC

Merneptah Siptah, often simply referred to as Siptah, was the sixth pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty and ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1194 to 1188 BCE during the New Kingdom period. Siptah's reign followed that of Seti II, and his ascent to the throne is somewhat obscure.
Siptah likely had a physical disability, and his reign was likely influenced by powerful figures at court, notably his regent and eventual successor, Tawosret. Siptah's rule faced internal challenges, including political intrigues and rival factions within the royal court.
During his reign, Siptah initiated some construction projects, including additions to the temple at Karnak. However, the political unrest persisted, and it continued into the subsequent reign of Queen Tawosret, who eventually assumed the throne after Siptah's death.
Siptah's rule is marked by its brevity, political complexities, and the circumstances that paved the way for the following period in Egyptian history known as the Twentieth Dynasty. The events during his reign foreshadowed the decline of the New Kingdom and the beginning of the Third Intermediate Period.

1191–1190 BC

Tausret, also known as Twosret or Tawosret, was a queen and the last known ruler of the 19th Dynasty of ancient Egypt. She is thought to have reigned from approximately 1188 to 1186 BCE. Tausret is a significant historical figure because she assumed the throne after the death of her predecessor, Siptah, marking the end of the New Kingdom.
Tausret was initially the wife of Seti II and, upon his death, assumed the title of Pharaoh. Her reign faced challenges, including internal conflicts and external pressures. She struggled to assert her authority, and during her rule, a powerful official named Bay played a prominent role as a regent.
Tausret's reign was relatively short and marked by political instability. Eventually, Setnakhte, a military leader, seized power, bringing an end to Tausret's rule. Setnakhte established the 20th Dynasty, and his son, Ramesses III, would go on to become one of the last great pharaohs of the New Kingdom.
Tausret's historical legacy is notable for being one of the few instances of a woman assuming the full kingship in ancient Egypt, but her reign and its aftermath are also indicative of the declining power and stability of the New Kingdom.

1190–1186 BC

Setnakhte, also spelled Setnakht, was the founder of the 20th Dynasty and the father of Ramesses III. He ruled over ancient Egypt from around 1186 to 1184 BCE during the turbulent period that marked the transition from the New Kingdom to the Third Intermediate Period.
Setnakhte's rise to power was not straightforward, as he came from a military background. He seized the throne after a time of political instability that included the brief reign of Tausret and the end of the 19th Dynasty. Setnakhte is often considered to have played a role in restoring order and stability to the kingdom.
Despite the brevity of his reign, Setnakhte undertook some construction projects, and he is mentioned in the historical records as a ruler who sought to reaffirm the authority of the central government. His son, Ramesses III, succeeded him, and together, they played crucial roles in shaping the political landscape during the later New Kingdom and the subsequent Third Intermediate Period in ancient Egyptian history.

1186–1155 BC

Ramesses III, the second pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1184 to 1153 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded his father Setnakhte and is often regarded as one of the last great pharaohs of the New Kingdom.
Ramesses III's reign faced significant challenges, including invasions by the Sea Peoples, a coalition of different groups who attacked the eastern Mediterranean and the coastal regions of the Near East. Ramesses III successfully repelled these invasions and is credited with securing Egypt's borders. He documented these victories in the Medinet Habu reliefs, providing valuable historical insights into the events of his time.
In addition to his military achievements, Ramesses III contributed to the construction of temples and monuments, including the completion of the Temple of Karnak. He also commissioned the construction of his mortuary temple at Medinet Habu, which is one of the best-preserved temples in Egypt.
Towards the end of his reign, Ramesses III faced internal challenges, including conspiracies and attempts on his life. A plot known as the Harem Conspiracy sought to place a rival on the throne, but the pharaoh's security measures thwarted the conspiracy. Ramesses III's death is shrouded in mystery, and there are theories suggesting that he might have been a victim of an assassination.
Despite the challenges and uncertainties surrounding the end of his reign, Ramesses III left a lasting impact on Egypt, both in terms of military successes and architectural accomplishments. His rule marked the closing chapter of the New Kingdom and the beginning of the decline that would characterize the subsequent Third Intermediate Period.

1155–1149 BC

Ramesses IV, the third pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1153 to 1147 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He was the son of Ramesses III and ascended to the throne after the death of his father.
Ramesses IV's reign is known for its emphasis on economic and administrative reforms. He undertook various building projects, including the completion of the Great Hypostyle Hall at the Karnak Temple, which had been initiated by his father. Additionally, he worked on his mortuary temple, known as the Ramesseum, on the west bank of the Nile near Luxor.
During Ramesses IV's rule, Egypt faced economic challenges, including the diminishing resources of the state. He implemented measures to address these issues, such as imposing taxes and reducing expenditures. The king's efforts to stabilize the economy are documented in the Wilbour Papyrus.
Ramesses IV's reign was relatively short, and he was succeeded by his son, Ramesses V. While not as prominent as some of his predecessors, Ramesses IV's contributions to the economy and his building projects added to the overall legacy of the Ramesside period in ancient Egyptian history.

1149–1145 BC

Ramesses V, the fourth pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt for a relatively short period, from approximately 1147 to 1143 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded his father, Ramesses IV, to the throne.
The reign of Ramesses V is somewhat obscure, and not much is known about his accomplishments. The limited historical records indicate that he faced challenges, perhaps including economic difficulties, as attested by the Wilbour Papyrus, which also details his efforts to address these issues.
Ramesses V was buried in the Valley of the Kings (KV9), but the exact circumstances of his death and the events surrounding the end of his reign are not well-documented. His successor, Ramesses VI, continued the efforts to stabilize the economy and address the challenges facing the kingdom.
The Ramesside period, encompassing the reigns of Ramesses III through Ramesses XI, was marked by economic troubles, political instability, and a decline in the central authority of the pharaohs. It paved the way for the subsequent Third Intermediate Period in ancient Egyptian history.

1145–1137 BC

Ramesses VI, the fifth pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1143 to 1136 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded his predecessor Ramesses V to the throne.
Ramesses VI's reign faced continued economic challenges and internal issues that characterized the later Ramesside period. The king's efforts to address these challenges included various economic reforms and initiatives, as seen in the Turin Strike Papyrus, which recorded labor disputes and strikes during his rule.
One of the notable features of Ramesses VI's reign is his tomb, located in the Valley of the Kings (KV9). The tomb is known for its extensive decorations and scenes from the Book of Gates, Book of Caverns, and other funerary texts, providing valuable insights into the religious beliefs and rituals of the time.
Despite Ramesses VI's efforts, the economic and political challenges facing Egypt persisted, contributing to the overall decline of the New Kingdom. After his death, the Ramesside Dynasty continued with Ramesses VII taking the throne. The later rulers of the New Kingdom faced increasing difficulties, leading to the eventual breakdown of central authority and the beginning of the Third Intermediate Period in ancient Egyptian history.

1137–1130 BC

Ramesses VII, the sixth pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt for a relatively short period, from approximately 1136 to 1131 BCE, during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded Ramesses VI to the throne.
Ramesses VII's reign, like those of his predecessors in the later Ramesside period, was marked by economic challenges and internal issues. While historical records from this time are limited, it is evident that the central authority of the pharaohs continued to decline.
The exact circumstances of Ramesses VII's death and the events surrounding the end of his reign are not well-documented. Following his rule, the Ramesside Dynasty continued with the ascension of Ramesses VIII, continuing the pattern of relatively short and less impactful reigns during this later period of ancient Egyptian history.
The decline of the New Kingdom and the challenges faced by the Ramesside pharaohs set the stage for the subsequent Third Intermediate Period, characterized by political fragmentation, foreign invasions, and shifts in the political landscape of ancient Egypt.

1130–1129 BC

Ramesses VIII, the seventh pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt for a relatively brief period, from approximately 1131 to 1125 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded Ramesses VII to the throne.
The reign of Ramesses VIII is poorly documented, and historical records about his rule are scarce. The decline of the New Kingdom continued during his time, marked by economic difficulties, internal challenges, and a diminishing central authority. The exact circumstances of his death and the events surrounding the end of his reign remain unclear.
After Ramesses VIII, the Ramesside Dynasty continued with the rule of Ramesses IX, and the subsequent pharaohs faced similar challenges. The Third Intermediate Period followed, characterized by political fragmentation and a decline in the centralized power of the pharaohs.
The later years of the New Kingdom and the Ramesside period represent a complex and transitional phase in ancient Egyptian history, marked by internal struggles, economic troubles, and external pressures that contributed to the eventual decline of the once-great New Kingdom.

1129–1111 BC

Ramesses IX, the eighth pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt from approximately 1125 to 1107 BCE during the New Kingdom period. He succeeded Ramesses VIII to the throne.
The reign of Ramesses IX continued to be characterized by the challenges that had plagued his predecessors during the later Ramesside period. Economic difficulties, internal unrest, and a diminishing central authority persisted. The historical records from this time are limited, and detailed information about specific events during Ramesses IX's rule is scarce.
One notable aspect of Ramesses IX's reign is the extensive plundering and reuse of monuments and tombs from earlier periods. This practice reflects the economic difficulties faced by the pharaohs of the declining New Kingdom.
After Ramesses IX, the Ramesside Dynasty continued with rulers like Ramesses X, Ramesses XI, and others. The later years of the 20th Dynasty marked the end of the New Kingdom, leading to the subsequent Third Intermediate Period. During this time, Egypt experienced political fragmentation, invasions, and shifts in power that marked the beginning of a new era in its long history.

1111–1107 BC

Ramesses X, the ninth pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt during the later years of the New Kingdom, from approximately 1107 to 1078 BCE. His reign was marked by the continued decline of central authority and economic difficulties that characterized the later Ramesside period.
Historical records from this time are limited, and detailed information about specific events during Ramesses X's rule is scarce. The weakening of the central government led to increased local autonomy and challenges to the traditional pharaonic authority.
Following Ramesses X, the decline of the New Kingdom continued with the rule of subsequent pharaohs such as Ramesses XI. The end of the 20th Dynasty marked the close of the New Kingdom, and the subsequent Third Intermediate Period saw significant political changes, including the fragmentation of central authority, foreign invasions, and shifts in the power dynamics of ancient Egypt. This period laid the groundwork for the later reunification of Egypt under the rule of the first pharaoh of the 21st Dynasty, Smendes.

1107–1077 BC

Ramesses XI, the tenth and last pharaoh of the 20th Dynasty, ruled over ancient Egypt during a tumultuous period from approximately 1078 to 1070 BCE, marking the end of the New Kingdom. His reign faced significant challenges, including internal strife, economic troubles, and the weakening of central authority.
During Ramesses XI's rule, the authority of the pharaohs continued to decline, and the country experienced increased fragmentation. The High Priests of Amun, particularly Herihor and later Piankh, gained significant power and influence, essentially functioning as de facto rulers in Thebes.
The declining power of the central government and the division of authority between the pharaoh and the High Priests are evident in the so-called "Great Harris Papyrus," which details the distribution of land and privileges among the various priests and officials.
The end of Ramesses XI's reign marked the conclusion of the New Kingdom and the beginning of the subsequent Third Intermediate Period, characterized by political fragmentation, invasions, and significant changes in the socio-political landscape of ancient Egypt. The era saw the rise of regional rulers and the establishment of the Twenty-First Dynasty, marking the start of a new chapter in Egyptian history.

1077–664 BC

The Third Intermediate period occurred after the death of Ramesses XI. It served as a time of many changes in Egypt and its details to this day remain unclear.
